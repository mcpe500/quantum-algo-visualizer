# Report Writing Guidelines

Use this guide whenever you ask for a report. A good report should be easy to understand, quick to scan, and useful for decision-making.

---

## 1. Start With the Purpose

Before writing, define:

- **Audience:** Who is reading this?
- **Goal:** What should the reader understand or decide?
- **Use case:** Is this for executives, clients, internal planning, analysis, or documentation?
- **Desired action:** What should happen after they read it?

A report without a clear purpose usually becomes too long, too vague, or too detailed.

---

## 2. Lead With the Answer

Do not make the reader search for the point.

Start with:

1. **Main conclusion**
2. **Key findings**
3. **Recommended next steps**

The reader should understand the core message within the first 30 seconds.

Good opening structure:

```md
## Executive Summary

The main finding is...
This matters because...
The recommended action is...
```

---

## 3. Use a Clear Structure

A strong report usually follows this order:

```md
# Report Title

## Executive Summary
Brief summary of the conclusion, findings, and recommendation.

## Context
Why this report exists and what question it answers.

## Key Findings
The most important insights, grouped clearly.

## Analysis
Evidence, reasoning, comparisons, examples, or data.

## Recommendations
Specific actions the reader should take.

## Risks / Considerations
Important caveats, tradeoffs, or uncertainties.

## Next Steps
Concrete follow-up actions, owners, or timeline.

## Appendix
Extra details, data, sources, or supporting material.
```

Keep the main report focused. Put extra details in the appendix.

---

## 4. Make It Easy to Scan

Most people skim reports before reading deeply.

Use:

- Short sections
- Clear headings
- Bullet points
- Tables when comparing things
- Bold text for important takeaways
- Short paragraphs

Avoid long walls of text.

A good rule: if a paragraph is longer than 5 lines, split it.

---

## 5. Use Plain Language

Write so that a smart but busy person can understand it quickly.

Prefer:

- “Use” instead of “utilize”
- “Because” instead of “due to the fact that”
- “Help” instead of “facilitate”
- “Show” instead of “demonstrate”

Avoid jargon unless the audience already knows it.

If technical terms are necessary, explain them briefly.

---

## 6. Separate Facts, Interpretation, and Recommendations

A good report makes it clear what is known, what it means, and what should be done.

Use this pattern:

```md
### Finding
What happened or what the data shows.

### Interpretation
Why it matters.

### Recommendation
What should be done next.
```

Example:

```md
### Finding
Customer support tickets increased by 28% in March.

### Interpretation
The increase is mostly related to billing confusion after the pricing update.

### Recommendation
Update the billing FAQ and add a pricing explanation inside the onboarding flow.
```

---

## 7. Focus on the Few Most Important Points

A report should not include every detail.

Prioritize:

1. What changes the decision
2. What explains the situation
3. What the reader needs to act

Remove anything that is merely interesting but not useful.

A good report usually has **3 to 5 key findings**, not 15.

---

## 8. Use Strong Headings

Headings should communicate meaning, not just label sections.

Weak heading:

```md
## Data
```

Better heading:

```md
## Usage Increased After the New Onboarding Flow
```

Weak heading:

```md
## Problems
```

Better heading:

```md
## The Main Risk Is Low Retention After Week Two
```

Readers should be able to skim the headings and understand the story.

---

## 9. Include Evidence, But Do Not Overload the Reader

Support your claims with:

- Data
- Examples
- Quotes
- Screenshots
- Benchmarks
- Source references

But keep the main report clean.

Use this pattern:

```md
**Claim:** Trial users are dropping off before setup is complete.

**Evidence:** 43% of new users exit during the workspace setup step.

**Implication:** Improving setup completion should increase activation.
```

---

## 10. Make Recommendations Specific

Avoid vague recommendations like:

- “Improve communication”
- “Optimize the process”
- “Increase engagement”

Use specific recommendations:

```md
- Add a setup checklist to the onboarding page.
- Send a reminder email 24 hours after signup if setup is incomplete.
- Create a billing FAQ linked from the pricing page.
```

A good recommendation includes:

- What to do
- Why it matters
- Expected impact
- Difficulty or priority
- Owner or next step, when relevant

---

## 11. Use Tables for Comparisons

Tables are helpful when comparing options, tradeoffs, priorities, or recommendations.

Example:

```md
| Option | Benefit | Risk | Effort | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Option A | Fast to launch | Limited scope | Low | Use first |
| Option B | More complete | Takes longer | Medium | Use later |
| Option C | Highest impact | Most complex | High | Consider after validation |
```

---

## 12. Make the Report Visually Clean

Good formatting makes the report feel easier to read.

Use:

- Consistent heading levels
- Enough spacing
- Simple tables
- Short bullets
- Minimal bolding
- Clear labels

Avoid:

- Too many font styles
- Too many colors
- Huge blocks of text
- Overly complex charts
- Decorative visuals that do not help understanding

---

## 13. Add an Executive Summary

Every important report should have an executive summary.

A good executive summary answers:

1. What is the situation?
2. What did we find?
3. Why does it matter?
4. What should we do?

Template:

```md
## Executive Summary

This report examines [topic] to understand [main question].

The main finding is that [key conclusion].

The most important supporting points are:
- [Finding 1]
- [Finding 2]
- [Finding 3]

This matters because [business/user/team impact].

The recommended next step is to [specific action].
```

---

## 14. Add a “So What?” After Each Major Point

For every finding, ask:

> So what?

If the answer is unclear, the point may not belong in the report.

Example:

```md
Finding: Mobile traffic increased by 35%.

So what: The current signup page performs poorly on mobile, so improving mobile conversion could have a large impact.
```

---

## 15. Be Honest About Uncertainty

Do not make weak evidence sound certain.

Use clear uncertainty language:

- “The data suggests...”
- “This may indicate...”
- “The evidence is limited because...”
- “More validation is needed before...”

Include caveats when they matter.

Example:

```md
This conclusion is based on a small sample of 18 interviews, so it should be treated as directional rather than definitive.
```

---

## 16. End With Concrete Next Steps

Do not end with analysis only.

End with action.

Example:

```md
## Next Steps

1. Confirm the analysis with the product analytics team.
2. Prioritize the onboarding checklist experiment.
3. Draft updated billing FAQ copy.
4. Review results two weeks after launch.
```

When useful, include owners and dates:

```md
| Step | Owner | Due Date |
|---|---|---|
| Draft FAQ copy | Marketing | May 3 |
| Implement onboarding checklist | Product | May 10 |
| Review experiment results | Analytics | May 24 |
```

---

## 17. Recommended Report Style

Aim for a report that is:

- **Clear:** easy to understand
- **Structured:** logical flow
- **Brief:** no unnecessary detail
- **Evidence-based:** claims are supported
- **Actionable:** includes recommendations
- **Skimmable:** easy to scan quickly
- **Honest:** clear about limits and uncertainty

---

## 18. Final Quality Checklist

Before finishing a report, check:

- [ ] Is the purpose clear?
- [ ] Is the audience clear?
- [ ] Does the executive summary give the main answer?
- [ ] Are the key findings easy to scan?
- [ ] Are claims supported by evidence?
- [ ] Are recommendations specific?
- [ ] Are risks or caveats included?
- [ ] Are next steps clear?
- [ ] Is the report free of unnecessary detail?
- [ ] Could a busy reader understand the main point in 30 seconds?

---

## 19. Default Prompt to Create a Good Report

Use this prompt when asking for a report:

```md
Create a clear, easy-to-digest report about [topic].

Audience: [who will read it]
Purpose: [what decision or understanding the report should support]
Tone: clear, concise, professional
Length: [short / medium / detailed]
Format: Markdown

Please include:
- Executive summary
- Context
- Key findings
- Analysis
- Recommendations
- Risks or caveats
- Next steps

Make it easy to scan, avoid jargon, and lead with the main conclusion.
```
